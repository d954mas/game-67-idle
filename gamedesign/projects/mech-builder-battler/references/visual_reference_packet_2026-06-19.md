## Visual Positioning

Working target:

> Stylized 3D garage-and-arena mech fantasy: chunky readable machines,
> satisfying modular parts, bright combat feedback, and a clean mobile HUD.

The game should sit between:

- Mech Arena: mech action/hangar clarity.
- CATS: object-centered buildcraft and instant proof.
- Mechangelion: ultra-casual boss readability and simple buttons.
- War Robots: scale and weapon fantasy, used mostly as an upper-bound caution
  for clutter, PvP seriousness, and over-complex silhouettes.

## First-Screen Visual Contract

The first playable screen should answer, without text explanation:

1. This is my mech.
2. I can start a battle now.
3. One upgrade or locked slot shows future progress.
4. The mech has visible modular parts.
5. The interface is touch-first and not a PC sim panel.

Required first-screen composition:

- Large 3D mech, occupying roughly 45-60% of the safe viewport height.
- Three-quarter hangar camera, slightly low or eye-level to make the mech feel
  powerful but still readable.
- Calm industrial hangar background with depth, not a busy sci-fi city.
- Primary `Battle` action visually dominant.
- One visible part slot or upgrade prompt.
- Currency/resource counters only if they explain the next action.
- No offer wall, event carousel, mailbox stack, or roster grid before the first
  satisfying loop.

## 3D Mech Style

### Shape Language

- Player mech: broad torso, large shoulders, clear head/sensor block, readable
  arm weapon, distinct leg profile.
- Starter silhouette: compact hero mech, not a realistic tank on legs and not
  a fragile humanoid.
- Part silhouettes must be visible at phone size:
  - cannon arm: long barrel / recoil block,
  - rockets: shoulder pod / stacked tubes,
  - shield core: chest plate / forearm shield,
  - blade: glowing short-edge weapon,
  - heavy legs: wide feet / piston calves,
  - jump legs: slimmer calves / exposed boosters.

### Materials

- Primary material: painted metal panels with bevels large enough to catch
  light.
- Secondary material: dark mechanical joints, pistons, vents, undercarriage.
- Accent material: emissive reactor, sensors, weapon charge strips.
- Damage/readability: scuffs and scratches are acceptable, but no noisy
  micro-detail that turns into visual mush on mobile.

### Palette

Use a restrained, function-coded palette rather than one dominant hue:

- Player base: graphite, off-white, steel blue, or muted red panels.
- Energy accents: cyan or electric blue.
- Heat/danger: amber, orange, or red.
- Enemy threat: red/magenta lights or warning shapes.
- Rewards: gold, green, or bright salvage glow.
- Hangar: neutral gray metal with small warm work lights.

Avoid a one-note purple/blue sci-fi gradient, all-brown industrial grime, or a
dark slate screen where the mech disappears.

## Hangar Composition

Borrow:

- Mech Arena's hangar-as-home fantasy.
- CATS' central owned-object focus.
- Clear upgrade surfaces attached to the object, not hidden in dense menus.

Adaptation:

- One mech first, not a five-mech squad lineup.
- Use 3-5 large part hotspots around the mech.
- Keep camera orbit/inspection as a later nice-to-have; first proof only needs
  one strong idle animation and visible part swap.
- Use icon-led controls: weapon, legs, module, paint, upgrade, battle.

Avoid:

- Event crate buttons, shop tabs, tournament banners, inbox badges, and daily
  offer stacks in the first screen.
- Simulator-style grids of tiny stat rows.
- Exact Mech Arena hangar layout or CATS garage layout.

## Battle Composition

Recommended first battle view:

- Small 3D arena with fixed or gently following three-quarter camera.
- Player mech lower center or lower left, enemy wave in upper/mid space.
- Enemy and objective readable without precise aim.
- HUD safe areas:
  - left lower: movement or drag zone,
  - right lower: primary/special/defense buttons,
  - top center: enemy/boss health or wave progress,
  - top corners: minimal resources/objective only if needed.

First battle must show the player mech often. Do not zoom so close that the
model ownership fantasy disappears, and do not pull so far away that parts are
unreadable.

## Enemy Silhouettes

First-slice enemy families:

| Enemy | Shape | Visual Job | Gameplay Job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Drone swarm | tiny hovering triangles/discs with one red eye | Shows area-damage value | Dies fast, teaches rockets or sweeping attack |
| Shield guard | squat rectangle with front shield plate | Teaches armor/weak side | Needs flank, shield break, or heavy hit |
| Sniper turret | tall thin tripod/tower with glowing barrel | Teaches danger line and dash | Fires telegraphed high damage |
| Charger | low wedge body with bright warning horns/lights | Teaches timing and defense | Rushes, can be interrupted |
| Mini-boss | 2-3x player height or width, clear weak points | Sells spectacle | Tests weapon + defense + movement |

Rules:

- Enemy role must be visible from silhouette before reading labels.
- Enemy colors should separate from player accents.
- Do not make early enemies look cooler or more customizable than the player
  mech.
- Boss scale can borrow from Mechangelion, but not its exact dinosaur/robot
  designs.

## Effects And Phone-Scale Readability

Effects should communicate function:

- Hit: short yellow/white spark burst and small knockback.
- Shield: cyan arc or translucent panel, not a full-screen bloom.
- Heat: amber glow on weapon/vents, red warning only near danger.
- Rockets: orange muzzle flash, smoke trail, compact explosion.
- Laser/beam: thin bright core with brief afterglow.
- Reward: gold/green salvage magnetizes toward the player or reward counter.
- Upgrade: part socket glow, bolt-on animation, before/after flash.

Limits:

- No effect should cover the mech silhouette for more than a short beat.
- Avoid dense particle fog, screen shake spam, and overlapping damage numbers.
- Damage numbers are optional; if used, keep them large, brief, and few.
- Use color coding consistently across hangar, battle, and rewards.

## UI Visual Rules

- Buttons are large, icon-led, and spaced for touch.
- Text labels are short and secondary to symbols.
- Primary action has one clear location.
- Locked states are visible but not louder than the available next action.
- Upgrade comparison should show one meaningful visual/mechanical change, not
  a wall of stat text.
- Cards may be used for repeated inventory/part items, but do not nest cards
  inside cards or make the whole hangar a card stack.

## Borrow / Avoid / Copy-Risk

### Borrow

- Mech Arena: readable battle HUD zones, hangar ownership, special abilities,
  short-session mech action.
- CATS: central built object, instant build proof, playful part readability.
- Mechangelion: large boss scale, bright casual readability, very few combat
  actions.
- War Robots: weapon fantasy, robot role variety, environmental scale.

### Avoid

- Mech Arena/War Robots service clutter in the first screen.
- CATS' exact vehicle/cat grammar.
- Mechangelion's generic punching-only robot fantasy.
- Dark realistic mech kitbash that reads as noise on mobile.
- UI dominated by currencies, offers, events, inboxes, and upgrade popups.
- Effects that hide the model or target.

### Copy-Risk

- Exact robot silhouettes, faction/brand marks, names, skins, UI hierarchy,
  event crate shapes, CATS cat/vehicle theme, Mechangelion boss designs,
  War Robots horned/weapon silhouettes, and store screenshot compositions.

## First Fake-Shot Requirements

Before final assets, produce three fake shots or equivalent mock screens:

1. Hangar first screen:
   - one large 3D mech,
   - one `Battle` action,
   - visible part slots,
   - one locked/progress hint,
   - no monetization clutter.
2. Battle moment:
   - player mech readable,
   - one enemy role readable,
   - one special effect active,
   - touch-friendly controls,
   - top progress/health readable.
3. Reward/upgrade return:
   - salvage or part reward,
   - clear slot destination,
   - visual before/after or bolt-on animation concept,
   - next battle prompt.

Acceptance for fake shots:

- A phone-scale screenshot should answer where the player is, what to do, what
  changed, and why the mech is worth upgrading.
- The mech silhouette must remain readable at small size.
- Effects and HUD must not overlap incoherently.
- The shot should look like a game screen, not a technical viewer or debug
  layout.
