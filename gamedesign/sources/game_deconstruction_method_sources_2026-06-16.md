## Method takeaways (what real teardowns do)

- **Deconstructor of Fun pattern (secondary):** a good teardown is evidence-based
  and covers the **core loop, the meta/economy, retention drivers, and
  monetization** — not a feature list. Study their published deconstructions of
  a specific game as worked examples before claiming you "deconstructed" one.
- **Emotion / engagement (Sensemitter, observed):** beyond systems, deconstruct
  *why players disengage* — onboarding timing, friction, the moment-to-moment
  emotional arc. Pairs with our teachability + core-loop gates.
- **Fun = learning/mastery (Koster, secondary):** a loop is "fun" when it keeps
  teaching the player something to master; a screen that looks like a game but
  teaches/offers nothing is not fun. This underpins our `not-fun = not-done`.

## How this grounds our method

`reference_deconstruction.md` already demands a source matrix, an observation
ledger (>=5 beats), screen grammar, economy/numbers, borrow/avoid/copy-risk, a
mismatch audit, and evidence labels. These external sources confirm that bar:
- a deconstruction needs **observed gameplay evidence** (Deconstructor of Fun
  works from actual play, not store copy);
- it must reach the **economy + retention + emotional arc**, not just mechanics;
- "fun" is a real, judgeable property (Koster), so the core-loop gate is not
  optional polish.

Honesty note for OUR studies: when we research from web text/wiki/walkthrough
(no first-hand video frames), label claims `secondary`/`inferred` and mark the
source packet incomplete — do not write "observed" for evidence we did not see.
