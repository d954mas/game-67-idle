# Product Vision

## Goal

Build a compact fantasy RPG that can start as a small playable slice and grow into a long-running adventure game without changing its core loop.

The core must be compelling immediately:

```text
dangerous route -> meaningful choice -> visible risk -> loot or clue -> camp recovery/prep -> new route
```

The long-term game should scale by adding regions, encounters, companions, factions, items, perks, and dragon mystery beats, not by replacing the core with unrelated base-building or idle systems.

## Player Promise

In a short session, the player should feel:

- I chose where to go.
- I found something dangerous or strange.
- I made a decision that changed my state.
- I returned to camp with a reason to prepare better.
- The world opened one step further.

## Target Audience

- Players who like Skyrim/Elder Scrolls fantasy, but need a compact mobile/PC format.
- Players who like old-school first-person RPG structure, but want modern readability.
- Players who like survival pressure, but not heavy punishment or spreadsheet clutter.
- Players who enjoy companions, camp conversations, and mystery progression.

## Product Shape

- Session length: 2-5 minutes for a useful beat, 10-20 minutes for a small expedition.
- Camera/presentation: illustrated first-person encounters plus node map.
- Main rhythm: exploration first, camp second.
- Monetization: not designed yet; do not add monetization until the core loop is proven.
- Platform: mobile portrait and PC; PC is preferred for development automation.

## Long-Term Fantasy

The hero begins as a local adventurer and becomes a province-level mythic figure tied to an ancient dragon mystery.

Progression should be visible through:

- unlocked regions;
- better equipment;
- stronger perks;
- deeper companion trust;
- camp becoming more capable but not a base-builder;
- dragon omen evolving from clue to power;
- faction reputation changing routes and quest outcomes.

## Strategic Pillars

### 1. One More Route

The strongest retention hook is not a timer. It is curiosity: a locked bridge, a rumor, a ruin silhouette, a companion warning, a dragon mark.

### 2. Camp Means Preparation

Camp should make the next expedition more tempting. It should never become a separate management game.

### 3. Small Systems, Many Combinations

The game scales by recombining simple parts:

- route type;
- encounter type;
- danger type;
- reward type;
- camp response;
- unlock result.

### 4. Readable RPG Depth

Every number should answer a player question:

- Can I survive?
- Can I risk this route?
- What did I gain?
- What changed after this upgrade?
- Why should I return to camp?

## Future Product Goals

### P0: First Playable Proof

One route, one ruin, one enemy, one reward, one camp beat, one unlock.

### P1: Small Adventure Loop

Three routes, two enemy types, two companions, one faction hint, one mini-boss, first dragon omen milestone.

### P2: Region Loop

One full region with 8-12 nodes, 3 companion beats, 2 factions, gear/crafting growth, and a region climax.

### P3: Content Scaling

Repeatable content templates allow new regions, quests, enemies, perks, loot, camp scenes, and dragon beats without redesigning the core.

## What Must Stay True

- Exploration is the primary activity.
- Camp supports exploration.
- Dragon progression is mystery/power, not gacha collection.
- Survival pressure creates decisions, not punishment.
- New content should fit the same route/encounter/camp/unlock loop.
